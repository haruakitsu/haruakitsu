✦ The Fox That Stayed

Haru never felt like he belonged inside large groups of people.

It was not because he hated people. He simply felt overwhelmed by too many voices, too much attention and the constant pressure to react.

But when Haru truly became attached to someone, he loved deeply. Almost too deeply.

Because of that, he rarely allowed people to get close. Being alone felt safer than losing someone important again.

Music, drawing, evening walks and sunset skies slowly replaced real closeness in his life. Haru got used to loneliness — not because he wanted it, but because it became familiar.

Then, one day, he realized something that scared him: he no longer felt like a lonely person. He felt like someone who had existed alone for far too long.

The fox appeared during a rainy evening.

Quiet. Still. Strange.

It did not run away. It did not come closer either. It simply sat nearby, as if it already understood him.

Haru kept seeing the fox again — near empty stations, under street lights, in window reflections and between falling sakura petals.

The fox never made a sound. But near it, Haru no longer felt emotionally empty.

Eventually, Haru understood something strange: the fox was lonely too.

Not like an animal. Like a spirit that had wandered alone for too long.

Maybe that was why it stayed near him. Maybe it recognized the same loneliness inside Haru.

Then, one night, the fox disappeared completely. Haru never saw it separately again.

But after that, the changes began.

His emotions became sharper. He noticed warmth in voices, silence between words, the feeling of rain and the color of sunset light on windows.

And later — fox ears and a tail appeared.

At first, Haru tried to understand what had happened to him.

But eventually he realized:

The fox had not truly disappeared.

It simply stopped existing separately from him.

“Some fox spirits stay beside lonely people until they no longer have to carry that loneliness alone.”
